Every story has a beginning and an end. What lies between those two points is the journey. R.C Richter

Having grown up in Colorado, my family always had loose connections to Germany. From a family recipe for cottage cheese dumplings called "Käsmolish" (this was the first time I had ever tried to write that word) to a grandmother who spoke a small amount of German. But for 22 years, I had never met anyone from Germany until I began my graduate studies in Vancouver, CA. I quickly found myself with a German roommate, friends from my studies and before I knew it, most of my friends were German. This was my first sign I would move to Germany - I seemed to get along well with Germans!
With all of this German influence, I slowly learned about various German traditions: Glühwein around Christmas, Spätzle with bigger dinners, How to drink for Vatertag/Herrentag. Soon I found myself adoring traditions I had never known, and looked forward to doing each year. Tradition and culture became important to me!

When I finished my master's, I took a many-month vacation to travel the world and learn cultures I had never been exposed to. I had begun in Japan, but even there I quickly found myself meeting large groups of Germans. Due to this, I spontaneously decided to take a month trip to Germany and stay on the couches of the people I met traveling - Berlin, Hamburg, Munich, Kaiserslautern. In each city I enjoyed learning what was unique about it, trying the local specialites and exploring the picturesque city-scape.

At the end of my travels, I took up a job in San Jose, California and started my career in tech but continued to keep close ties to those I had met in my journey. One day while I was out surfing, I met another German and again quickly made a friend. It was here I realized that I get along with Germans better than with Americans! If I had to pin point why, I'd believe it is the consistent open-minded and tolerant people I had met from Germany that resonated with me. Compound this with that deteriorating political and deplorable healthcare situations in the US, my choice to immigrate became clear.

After around 2 years in California starting 2020, I had officially decided that I must start making preparations. I began learning German through Duolingo and Deutche Welle (I'll address how I feel about these language learning tools in a future post) for about a year and investigating immigration pathways. Both luckily and unfortunately, 2020 also held the pandemic. The good news is that I has more time to plan my move, and I think we all know the bad news...

After much struggle, and many plans that fell through, I announced to my boss in June of 2021 that I would be leaving for Germany that August - it was the best move I ever made.
