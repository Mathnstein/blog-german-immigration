Every story has a beginning and an end. What lies between those two points is the journey. R.C Richter

Having grown up in Colorado, my family always had loose connections to Germany. From a family recipe for cottage cheese dumplings called "Käsmolish" (this was the first time I had ever tried to write that word) to a grandmother who spoke a small amount of German. But for 22 years, I had never met anyone from Germany until I began my graduate studies in Vancouver, CA. I quickly found myself with a German roommate, friends from my studies and before I knew it, most of my friends were German. I seemed to get along well with Germans!

With all of this German influence, I slowly learned about various German traditions: Glühwein around Christmas, Spätzle , How to drink for Vatertag/Herrentag. Soon I found myself adoring traditions I had never known, and looked forward to doing each year. Tradition and culture became important to me!

Once I finished my master's and with every penny I had saved during my program (roughly around 3,000 USD), I took a many-month vacation to travel the world and learn cultures I had never been exposed to. I had begun in Japan, but even there I quickly found myself meeting large groups of Germans. Due to this, I spontaneously decided to take a month trip to Germany and stay on the couches of the people I met traveling - Berlin, Hamburg, Munich, Kaiserslautern. In each city I enjoyed learning what was unique about it, trying the local specialites and exploring the picturesque city-scape. The cities seemed to be built with the individual in mind.



Work-centric life is draining...

At the end of my travels, I took up a job in San Jose, California and started my career in tech. Now in San Jose, much like most of the US, a car is absolutely essential to getting around the city, to work and even to the grocery store. The irony is I had been offered a substantial salary, and yet needed to borrow money from my mom in order to buy a car. Without knowing it, this was the first nail in the coffin: Owning a car is expensive both monetarily and environmentally while being rather annoying in California. At first I enjoyed the change in pace quite a bit, but when you spend an hour or more to get to work each day, you can feel your soul being drained.

While in Vancouver, I enjoyed the benefits of a public transit system actually designed for the individual. This is not the case either in Colorado or in California. It is an unfortunate truth with countless cases of transit systems being gutted by larger car companies to promote car sales. The big take away from my first year in San Jose was: This city was not designed for me.

Soon, I found myself traveling down to Santa Cruz nearly every weekend - a cute little beach town where surfing and skating still were strong in the peoples mind. I quickly made surf-friends in town and they showed me the ways of the waves, which became my primary hobby. Santa Cruz is not a big place, so getting around was much easier - in fact even walkable. But there was something else that drew me to the town - the people were real. Sure, I wasn't running into Tim Cook at the coffee shop or sneaking into Nvidia's $2 dinner or pride parade (before you ask, I had a roommate who was an intern there), but most importantly I was't being interviewed at house parties on what my job functions were anymore. Yes, you heard me - there was a strong sense that new people you met were evaluating your potential as a resource than as a friend in San Jose. This didn't stop at people I met in the city, it was emphisized greatly in my coworkers who couldn't talk about anything except work. Your worth was determined by your function.



So I moved...

Once my lease in San Jose was up, it was a no-brainer to move where I spent my time and I was even rather lucky as my office was the same distance away from where I had planned to move. I recall telling my boss my plans, which were already in motion might I add, and being asking if I would still be able to make it into work at the same frequency. Here was when I began to notice that my work did not care about my happiness.

I was given a "gracious" amount of time off given my work was in tech - around 18 days a year if I recall. Using this time off was another issue entirely, I would recieve strange looks and awkward pressure from my boss whenever I did ask. There was always justification required and when none of your coworkers/higher ups take time off, its hard to justify. But I kept in touch with my German friends, and they continued to point out to me that they had almost twice the amount of time off I did with none of this "justification" needed. They were getting 30 days off per year!



Side Topic: Heathcare...

Although this doesn't fit in with my work-centric discussion, I felt it was important to mention a random health care story that every American can relate to. When I first moved to California, I had two insurances. One with through my work and another from still being young enough to be on my family's plan. You would think I'm basically bullet-proof, right?

A friend and I had decided to go on a hike in the Santa Cruz mountains, and unfortunately a pretty heavy storm had rolled somewhere in the middle of this. It poured on us rather hard, so we ran back to the car and went home - it was during my shower I found a deer tick. Needless to say, that was not pleasant pulling that out.

Now I wouldn't have pursued this more but coincidentally I had also had quite the joint pain from a recent car-camping trip to see another friend in San Diego. With a deer tick comes the potential for Lyme disease, and that just happens to manifest itself as joint pain. So seeing a doctor was now a pretty good idea.

I went to a nearby urgent care, and had a subsequent blood panel to screen for the disease. Fortunately, there was no problems and life continued as normal.

Until 2 months had passed, where I recieved a bill for $800.

In Vancouver, I broke my shoulder snowboarding and paid nothing. I lived for 2 years in Canada without a single medical expense and now I have two health insurances that can't pay for a blood panel, let alone the panel costing an absurd amount.

I fought this bill for three months until it was dropped. Even the healthcare system was not designed for me.



Making the decision...

One day while I was out surfing, I met yet another German and again quickly made a friend. It was here I realized the final piece to the puzzle that had taken form in front of me: I get along with Germans better than with Americans! If I had to pin point why, I'd believe it is the consistent open-minded and tolerant people I had met from Germany that resonated with me. Compound this with how each public system around me had been designed to exploit and extract, and add the deteriorating political situation, my choice to immigrate became clear.

Starting in 2020, after around 2 years in California, I had officially decided that I must start making preparations. I began learning German through Duolingo and Deutche Welle (I'll address how I feel about these language learning tools in a future post) for about a year and investigating immigration pathways. Both luckily and unfortunately, 2020 also held the pandemic. The good news is that I has more time to plan my move, and I think we all know the bad news...

After much struggle, and many plans that fell through, I announced to my boss in June of 2021 that I would be leaving for Germany that August - it was the best move I ever made.
